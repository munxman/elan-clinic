How to Keep the Weight Off After Stopping GLP-1: A Physician's Six-Step Protocol

Dr. Ingmar Lindström
February 28, 2026
9 min read

Here is a number that should be part of every conversation about GLP-1 medications, but rarely is: 0.4 kilograms per month.

That is the average regain rate after stopping Ozempic or Wegovy, according to a 2026 BMJ meta-analysis that tracked thousands of people across multiple trials (West et al., BMJ 2026;392:e085304). At that pace, most of the weight that took months — sometimes years — of injections to lose comes back within about 18 months.

I am not telling you this to frighten you. I am telling you because the Ozempic Aftermath — what happens after the prescription ends — is something almost nobody talks about clearly, and you deserve the honest picture before you need it.

This is the six-step protocol we use at Élan Clinic for people who are planning to stop, or who have recently stopped, GLP-1 therapy. It is practical, evidence-based, and designed for real life — not a medical textbook.

Why the first 12 weeks are critical

When you stop a GLP-1 medication, your body does not gently return to its previous state. It snaps back. The drug was suppressing ghrelin (your hunger hormone), slowing gastric emptying, and sending satiety signals to your hypothalamus. When that pharmacological support is removed, all three systems reactivate — and they do not always recalibrate in a measured, polite way.

The BMJ data shows regain typically begins around eight weeks after the final injection and accelerates through a 20-week window. This is the period when habits either carry the load or do not.

GLP-1 was the scaffold. You have now removed it. The question is whether the building can stand on its own.

The six steps below are how you build that structure before the scaffold comes down — and how you reinforce it afterward.

The Six-Step Protocol

Step 1
Stop gradually, not overnight

If you have any say in how your treatment ends, do not stop abruptly. A structured taper over four to eight weeks significantly reduces the sharpness of the appetite rebound — because your brain gets time to adjust, rather than experiencing a sudden total withdrawal of the GLP-1 signal. The standard approach is to step down to half your usual dose for two to four weeks, then to a quarter dose, then stop. A full tapering protocol is covered separately in our article on stopping semaglutide correctly.

Step 2
Protein, and then more protein

GLP-1 medications are effective. They are also imprecise. When you lose weight on semaglutide or tirzepatide, roughly 25 to 30% of that weight loss comes from lean muscle mass rather than fat (STEP 1 extension trial, PubMed 35441470). The evidence supports a minimum of 1.6 grams per kilogram of body weight per day to preserve muscle mass. In practice, that means building three meals per day around a 30–40 gram protein anchor: eggs, cottage cheese, chicken, legumes, Greek yoghurt.

Step 3
Resistance training, twice a week, non-negotiable

A 2024 trial published in Frontiers in Endocrinology compared outcomes for people stopping GLP-1 therapy. Those who continued structured resistance training maintained significantly more of their weight loss at 12 months than those who did not. Cardio has benefits, but when the primary goal is weight maintenance, resistance training wins — because it directly addresses the muscle loss problem. Twice a week is the evidence-supported floor. Three times is better.

Step 4
Manage hunger actively

This is the step that catches people off guard. The practical tools for managing hunger without pharmacological support: structured meals every four to five hours (to prevent ghrelin spikes); soluble fibre at every meal (slows gastric emptying); and adequate sleep — seven to nine hours. Sleep deprivation measurably increases ghrelin and decreases leptin. If your sleep is poor, fixing it will do more for your hunger management than almost anything else on this list.

Step 5
Monitor your weight weekly, not daily

Daily weighing creates noise. Weight fluctuates by one to two kilograms naturally across a single day. Weekly weigh-ins — same time, same conditions (morning, after using the bathroom, before eating) — give you trend data. Establish a personal trigger point now: if your weight increases by two kilograms above your maintenance baseline, activate an intensified protocol. Two kilograms is correctable without drama. Ten kilograms is a different conversation.

Step 6
The environment does not fix itself

GLP-1 medications suppressed appetite. They did not change your kitchen, your habits, your relationship with food, or the environmental triggers that influenced your eating in the first place. That work still needs to happen — and now you have to do it without pharmaceutical support. A food environment audit is worth doing once: go through your kitchen and remove the high-calorie, low-satiety foods that you can eat without thinking. Friction works.

When to consider continued treatment

Here is something worth saying plainly: obesity is a chronic condition. For some people, the most medically appropriate long-term management includes continued GLP-1 therapy — intermittently, at a maintenance dose, or indefinitely. This is not a failure. It is how we manage most chronic conditions.

If you have followed this protocol conscientiously for three to six months and weight regain continues, the right conversation to have is not "what am I doing wrong?" It is "is ongoing medical treatment appropriate for me?" At Élan Clinic, we can help you evaluate that question without judgment.

The bottom line

Weight management is an infinite game — not a finite one you win once and walk away from. GLP-1 medications give you a significant advantage in that game for the duration of treatment. What happens after depends on the habits you build while that advantage is active.

The biology will work against you for roughly 20 weeks. The protocol above is designed to work against the biology. Use both the time and the tool you have been given well.
